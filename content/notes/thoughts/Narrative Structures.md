### Summary Table:

|Structure|Suitability for Horror|Strengths|Use Case|
|---|---|---|---|
|Dan Harmon’s Story Circle|⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐|Transformation, dread, repeatable|Campaigns with personal horror|
|Freytag’s Pyramid|⭐⭐⭐⭐|Climax fallout, emotional pacing|Gothic, tragic, or dramatic horror|
|Three-Act Structure|⭐⭐⭐|Clean, easy, one-shot friendly|Classic horror sessions|
|Hero’s Journey|⭐⭐|Epic scope, subversion potential|Dark fantasy horror|
|Seven-Point Structure|⭐|Plot precision|Avoid for atmospheric horror|


### **1. Dan Harmon’s Story Circle** — _Most effective for horror TTRPGs_

**Why:**  
The cyclical nature of the story circle mirrors how horror plays out: normalcy is disrupted, characters descend into chaos, and when they return, they’re no longer whole. The **"pay a heavy price"** and **"return changed"** steps naturally accommodate dread, madness, trauma, or loss—horror’s cornerstones. It also supports player-driven decisions within a loose arc.

**Tactical Benefits for a GM:**

- Flexible, repeatable across sessions
- Great for slow-burn horror with psychological depth
- Encourages character transformation and moral decay


### **2. Five-Act Structure (Freytag’s Pyramid)**

**Why:**  
This structure allows for a **long tension ramp** and a **devastating climax**, followed by **fallout**, which is crucial in horror. The **falling action and denouement** stages let you showcase how the horror echoes beyond the central conflict, ideal for campaigns where survival comes at a cost.

**Tactical Benefits for a GM:**

- Supports tragedy and moral ambiguity
- Works well with investigative or existential horror
- Gives room to spotlight consequences after the climax


### **3. Three-Act Structure**

**Why:**  
It’s serviceable, but it’s better for heroic arcs or clean resolution. Horror can use this model effectively—**especially in one-shots**—but it lacks the elasticity and moral murkiness of more layered structures.

**Tactical Benefits for a GM:**

- Easy to prep and run
- Useful for tight, closed scenarios (e.g., a haunted house)
- Not ideal for campaigns that require thematic escalation over time


### **4. Hero’s Journey**

**Why:**  
The Hero’s Journey emphasizes triumph and transformation. Horror usually strips away illusions rather than builds to empowerment. It can work if you're aiming for **cosmic horror** or **tragedy disguised as a heroic path**, but you’ll need to subvert key beats.

**Tactical Benefits for a GM:**

- Useful if horror is a _mask_ for a hero tale gone wrong
- Good for horror-fantasy hybrids
- But tends to resolve too cleanly unless inverted


### **5. Seven-Point Story Structure** — _Least fitting for horror TTRPGs_

**Why:**  
This structure is too plot-centric. Horror thrives on mood, atmosphere, and uncertainty, not tightly engineered plot turns. While the model supports tension spikes, it doesn't give dread time to metastasize.

**Tactical Downsides for a GM:**

- Too mechanical for horror’s emotional messiness
- Better for thrillers or mysteries with clear resolutions
- Doesn’t encourage the lingering unease horror demands
